Don’t Always Trust Your Sources
DON’T ALWAYS TRUST
YOUR SOURCES

Taking advantage of foreign companies who don’t know the real rules, Chinese manufacturers and deliverers often try to dupe foreigners by stating “such and such is Chinese law”: things like requiring the foreign company to transfer IP ownership or enter into exclusive distribution arrangeemtns with Chinese companies. There are no such laws for IP transfer or exclusivity requirements! In fact, it is extremely important not to enter into exclusivity arrangements with a distributor/marketer/seller because even if they are very good in one region of China, they may not be in another. In addition, if they have an exclusivity agreement with you, it is possible they have another agreement with a competitor company. They may simply choose not to sell your product so the other one doesn’t suffer from the competition, and because you signed X years of exclusivity, there is nothing you can do. Exclusive distribution is part of what led Hershey’s down the hole in China: its sales in 2015 were USD 90 million less than the projected USD 200 million, mostly because it had acquired a local candy maker company called Shanghai Golden Monkey to expand its local distributor network. But the distributor and customer network was not as broad or as stable as they had believed—and since they had spent USD 584 million acquiring the company, they were stuck with them.

Key Takeaways

Don’t trust what the companies say. Do your homework to confirm or deny their claims, especially about unfamiliar Chinese laws. And when in doubt, ask an expert.
