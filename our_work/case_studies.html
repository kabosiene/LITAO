Trouble With Customs Compliance
Trouble With
Customs Compliance

A US company’s product was denied entry by China’s inspection and quarantine bureau (AQSIQ) for six years straight. The bureau, which operates independently from the well-known customs administration (GACC), continually said the product was above its allowed limits for nitrate and nitrite—even though the importer and exporter tests both showed they were within limits. They finally had to hire a law firm to help them coordinate with a local inspection bureau so they could do identical product tests on both sides and compare results. However, the Chinese bureau’s standards allowed for only three testing methods, two of which weren’t even used in the US. They both agreed to do the tests using the remaining third method and identical materials, yielding results that finally allowed the company to ship to China. Without the law firm’s friendly relationship with the local inspection bureau, their products would still be languishing across the sea.

Key Takeaways

Even if you think you’re doing everything right, you may still be barred from entering China for reasons you can’t possibly know by yourself. Ask a local expert with connections. Inspection standards can vary depending on which testing methods are used.

A Long Process to Trade Approval
A Long Process
to Trade Approval

Getting a product approved for trade can be a laborious process, even when your country’s highest leaders approve and get involved. After Russian sanctions against EU products in 2014, Poland’s apple trade decreased almost 20% in a single year from 1.1 to 0.88 million tons. Russia had been 1/3 of its apple exports. To boost its apple trade, Poland applied for a trade agreement to import apples to China in August 2014. Fresh vegetables and fruits are especially difficult to be approved for import to China; even though they usually have fewer risks of contamination, the Chinese government treated with politically weighty fruit import with the procedural caution usually reserved for animal products. All grower hygiene methods and qualities had to be adjusted and inspected, and each apple had to be traced back to its origins. The trade agreement was finalized almost two years later in June 2016—this after a series of inspections, meetings between the two country’s heads of states, and after the Polish prime minister personally requested that the agreement be expedited. According to the Polish embassy in Beijing though, this was actually the FASTEST negotiation of this kind from EU states to the China market.

Key Takeaways

Regardless of the product, be prepared to wait at least a year for your product to be approved. If you’re not prepared for this, it could cause immense delays in your entry strategy. And remember: just meeting the regulations isn’t enough in China; it can also change them anytime. In this case, even though fruits and vegetables have less stringent quality inspection standards, the Chinese government treated them with the same standards as animal products. Regulations now are much stricter than a few decades ago, when China was just opening up and wanting to attract foreign companies. Now foreign companies are clamoring to enter, and China can pick and choose.

Apple: Costly Trademark Theft
Costly Trademark
Theft

China differs from the EU, the US and most other countries in the world for having a “first-to-file” trademark system. Companies like Apple have had to pay millions of dollars in court and settlement fees bcause Chinese companies can lay claim to patents even if they weren’t the first to develop a certain technology—as long as they were first to USE them in a Chinese market product. In July 2012, Apple paid $60 million to a small vendor for ownership of the “iPad” trademark, which the local Chinese company had previously registered. This delayed the introduction of the iPad 2 in China. More recently, another little-known startup won a case against Apple’s iPhone 6 and 6 Plus based on a smartphone design patent. Foreign companies often get entangled in intellectual property battles in China, where the courts proetect local intellectual property rights and the sheer number of companies means names aren’t guaranteed. China filed 12.5% more patent applications and 18.2% more trademark applications in 2015 compared to the year before, and this number is only growing. The same goes for domain names; when GroupOn tried to register as groupon.cn or groupon.com.cn, the names had already been registered by a copycat Chinese competitor, complete with the GroupOn company name, logo, and site design. It could do nothing about the copying, and also had to use a much less recognizable domain.

Key Takeaways

Protect your trademark from China’s first-to-file system as soon as you can, even before your company sets foot in China. In our experience, it usually takes around 18 months for a foreign company to successfully register a trademark—well worth it when you consider the time and money otherwise lost in court.

Pampers: Culturally Astute Advertising
PAMPERS: CULTURALLY
ASTUTE ADVERTISING

For a long time, the diapers industry did not take off in China—until 2007, when Proctor & Gamble launched an ad campaign linking diaper-wearing to better sleep—and research showing that this “golden sleep” meant better brain development. For a country where parents will do anything to ensure their kids’ success in school, sales went up 55%—and as another marketing boon, P&G received 200,000 photos of sleeping infants wearing their brand. Where else in the world could you sell diapers via a possibility of better test scores? That’s the power of culturally sensitive branding.

Key Takeaways

For memorable branding, talk about yourself in a way local people can understand. The Chinese don’t believe storks bring babies, so don’t advertise your products as such. Meanwhile, the Chinese ARE obsessed with their children’s education and test scores, so use that as a leverage point. Make the connection between your product and the culture you’re trying to sell to by finding out what they truly care about, and how you can help.

Star bucks : Selling the Experience
STARBUCKS: SELLING
THE EXPERIENCE

Sometimes the local culture doesn’t use your product at all. Branding is what gives you something else to offer. This is the by-now famous story of Starbucks: a coffee company trying to enter a thousand-year+ tea culture. Starbucks researchers realized they could sell not the coffee as a product itself, but an experience around it. They invested in prime, central business locations; leather armchairs, plush sofas, and other posh decor in a dimly lit environment; and outdoor seating with parasols for a European ambience. Businessmen could hold meetings in the private but bustling atmosphere; the status-conscious could sit and sip with their girlfriends in front of the large glass windows, taking selfies and hoping to be seen. Coffee as a status symbol of the West was perfect for a country with a growing upper-middle class that loves conspicuous consumption. Starbucks gave them more than a drink; it gave them a certain enviable experience and lifestyle. And China has now become its largest market outside of the U.S.

Key Takeaways

People don’t always want a product or service for itself—they want the good feelings and experience associated with it. See what unique perspectives you can offer and brand yourself that way, the way Starbucks, Pizza Hut, and Haagen-Dasz did to great success in China. They sold more than food and drink; they sold luxurious surroundings, status, and associations with the West. You can even sell soulfulness, as long as you find the right branding and niche.

Coca Cola: The Art Of Craftinga Chinese Name
THE ART OF CRAFTING
A CHINESE NAME

Everyone knows brand names and logos are important, but with Chinese social media famous for puns they can make out of various homonyms, the Chinese versions can truly make or break you. Creating a great brand name is not only a powerful marketing tool; if you don’t create it, you might not like what the Chinese consumers call it in its place. Two famous trademarks that did not make Chinese versions of their brand before entering the market met this fate. Chinese found it difficult to pronounce the phonetics of their foreign names in Chinese, so they called them by their logos: Quaker Oatmeal thus became “Lao Ren Pai,” which translates to “Old Man Brand,” and Ralph Lauren became “Three-Legged Horse.” Not exactly sexy branding. Meanwhile, Coca-cola is an early example of good branding at work; it was first sold in China in 1927, but local shopkeepers couldn’t figure out what to call it in China. They tried to find Chinese characters that sounded close to the name, coming up with a pool of 200 or so characters. But the names the shopkeepers came up with had strange definitions: though they were phonetically similar, the meanings varied from “female horse fastened with wax” to “bite the wax tadpole.” It was only a year later that Coca-Cola employees finally registered its current name: a combination of characters that means “to permit mouth to be able to rejoice”—a huge step up from horses and tadpoles the copying, and also had to use a much less recognizable domain.

Key Takeaways

Don’t wait for others to define you; since they don’t care about your reputation, their haphazard and sometimes disrespectful ideas are almost always going to be worse than ones you carefully craft with the help of local language experts. Choose a localized brand name with its associated meanings as carefully as choosing a name for a child, or as carefully you did the first time, since it’s your own corporate re-christening.

The Litao Approach To Cultural Integration
THE LITAO APPROACH TO
CULTURAL INTEGRATION

“Democracy” is the most celebrated play of Mindaugas Nastaravicius—a Lithuanian playwright who won a “Golden Cross of the Stage,” the highest award for theater in Lithuania. LITAO was approached to translate this play and adapt it for China for the 2016 Bookworm International Literary Festival.

The LITAO Approach:

True literary translation means cultural integration. Beyond language, we had to make sure ALL the elements of the play, from names and characters to jokes and cultural references, worked in China. Even the title of the play—Democracy—could attract unwanted political attention in a country like China. We changed it to an innocuous title in Chinese, 停电大楼, similar to “The Power is Out”—something intriguing without alerting the eyes of Chinese censors. The playwright had intended to translate simply the original concept and ideas—knowing that while his topics were universal, his references were not—and we worked with him closely to get his vision right but also relevant for China. His play featured Lithuanians in various social strata with personalities and characteristics familiar to Lithuanians; we adjusted that to prototypical characters from mainland China. A Russian Lithuanian therefore became a character from Northeast China, both known for their thick accents and alcoholic tolerance. All Catholic references were changed to Buddhist ones to better reflect local Chinese roots. We collaborated on all of this to the author’s satisfaction within only one(!) month.

The Outcomes

The Chinese reading of the play was well-received by audience members at the 2016 Bookworm Festival in Chengdu, China; the festival organizers were also extremely impressed at our culturally-astute adaptation. Subsequently, the playwright was approached by mainland China theaters offering to stage the play, as well as opportunities to publish it in Chinese.

The Importance of Interpreters
THE IMPORTANCE
OF INTERPRETERS

Interpretation is not to be taken lightly. In fact, Fortune Magazine has named interpreter Zhang Lu as one of the “world’s most powerful women.” She serves as the voice of China’s top leaders, including Premier Li Keqiang. In diplomatic situations, the interpreter’s voice stands as the voice of authority, and any deviation from both meaning and tone is an indictment on the nation itself. The U.S. Department of State had to learn this the hard way. When then-U.S. Secretary of State Hillary Clinton met Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov in 2009, she presented him a joke gift of a mock “reset” button—in symbolic honor of a relationship reset between the U.S. and Russia from tensions in the past. But the word the State Department chose for “reset” was the Russian “peregruzka,” which wrongly meant “overload”—the opposite of the meaning they were trying to convey (overload of tensions is never good), as Lavrov was quick to point out. The correct word for “reset” in Russian required an extra syllable: “perezagruzka.” If only the resources of the State Department had thought to first check the translation with a Russian expert!

Key Takeaways

Interpretation is a series of details and nuances that add up to a larger meaning such that you can barely notice the subtleties when it’s right, but you’ll sure notice when it’s missing. It pays to get the details right, especially for something as fickle as language. Hire a good interpreter, one who will be on your side and get across not just your literal meaning, but your intention.

Doing Things the Chinese Way : Guanxi
DOING THINGS THE
CHINESE WAY: GUANXI

In the early 1990’s, prospects were good for the growth of tourism and investment in Yunnan, a province with unique ethnicities and bountiful nature in Southwest China. The Roaring Dragon at that time was a well-known 40-year-old four-star hotel, one of the first in the region, owned by the provincial government and managed by local Chinese. But as a state-owned enterprise, their close to 700 staff lacked motivation since steady salaries would be paid for by the government in any case, and even though the hotel was still profitable, the government thought it wasn’t meeting its potential. And so the government contracted foreign management companies to take over: One takeover in 1993 lost them nine million RMB in a year. The second one in 1999, by an international hotel brand named Nothill, lost more than ten million RMB in less than four months, with hotel capacity nearing empty towards the end. In between these two periods, when Chinese management retook the system, the hotel did well again. It was clear that the foreign management did have better standards of services and more high-class hotel experience. But their international reputation did nothing to attract visitors; in fact, it sometimes seemed arrogant. Both times, foreign-educated managers neglected the Chinese element of guanxi, or connections. These connections always came with a price: they were relationships of “you help me, I help you.” Giving discounts to friends in tour agencies meant they would bring their groups to you. Hosting banquets in honor of government officials meant you could pass through bureaucratic processes much more smoothly. Hiring your friends or relatives meant they owed you something. The regular up-dating of these guanxi—meals, tea, gifts, even leisurely chats over nothing—were essential for relationship maintenance of mutual benefit..

Guanxi is effective, but it is not always efficient. Foreign managers at the Roaring Dragon saw it simply as favoritism or nepotism; they banned discounts, hiring based on connections, and other incentives based on guanxi. Once, a foreign F&B manager outright refused a tour group a banquet because he deemed it too cheap. The tour agency, which another Chinese manager had carefully cultivated guanxi with and which often booked large groups into the hotel, never booked tour groups in the hotel again. The guanxi was ruined, and the agent left to a competitor. Government officials were no longer given star treatment and discounts, so they left, too. Employees also complained that “the foreign management did not know the Chinese way”: they were no longer allowed to take long breaks, to invite their friends to the hotel for no reason, even to eat with each other as per new schedules. Long-time employees who were made redundant complained of not being treated with respect, since they were being laid off and paid on a single day without prior notice. Employee morale had depressed so much that even if they were not made redundant under new policies, many high-level employees left of their own accord, oftentimes with years of guanxi. Guanxi, because it is so personal, is something that is not transferable. In their quest to make everything more efficient, foreign managers had actually lost the very business and people they sought to attract.

Key Takeaways

Interpretation is a series of details and nuances that add up to a larger meaning such that you can barely notice the subtleties when it’s right, but you’ll sure notice when it’s missing. It pays to get the details right, especially for something as fickle as language. The LITAO Approach to an Exhibition

Flintas, a Lithuanian children’s books publisher, was entering the China International Children’s Book Fair in Shanghai for the first time. Though a thriving publisher in its home country, it didn’t know any of the requirements for China’s strict censorship laws and differentiations between publishers. LITAO was hired prior to and during the exhibition to prepare Flintas’ offerings and seek potential matches.

The LITAO Approach:

We first sorted through the publisher’s hundreds of titles to determine what would work best, settling on a total of twelve books marketed to children up to five years old. The publisher gained ~150 workable contacts through the exhibition and subsequently received its first copyright payments in less than a year—a remarkable outcome from a mere three-day presence at the fair.

Don’t Always Trust Your Sources
DON’T ALWAYS TRUST
YOUR SOURCES

Taking advantage of foreign companies who don’t know the real rules, Chinese manufacturers and deliverers often try to dupe foreigners by stating “such and such is Chinese law”: things like requiring the foreign company to transfer IP ownership or enter into exclusive distribution arrangeemtns with Chinese companies. There are no such laws for IP transfer or exclusivity requirements! In fact, it is extremely important not to enter into exclusivity arrangements with a distributor/marketer/seller because even if they are very good in one region of China, they may not be in another. In addition, if they have an exclusivity agreement with you, it is possible they have another agreement with a competitor company. They may simply choose not to sell your product so the other one doesn’t suffer from the competition, and because you signed X years of exclusivity, there is nothing you can do. Exclusive distribution is part of what led Hershey’s down the hole in China: its sales in 2015 were USD 90 million less than the projected USD 200 million, mostly because it had acquired a local candy maker company called Shanghai Golden Monkey to expand its local distributor network. But the distributor and customer network was not as broad or as stable as they had believed—and since they had spent USD 584 million acquiring the company, they were stuck with them.

Key Takeaways

Don’t trust what the companies say. Do your homework to confirm or deny their claims, especially about unfamiliar Chinese laws. And when in doubt, ask an expert.

Quality Fade Over Time
QUALITY FADE
OVER TIME

So you’ve got a trusted supplier with a good agreement. The initial production samples look good. But how can you insure they stay good? Quality fade—what happens when manufacturers go for wider margins by reducing product quality—happens gradually and often without notice. An American company that designed and patented alumninum building support systems supplied their parts from China. Since these support systems were used to construct high-rise buildings, they had sophisticated measurements for each part of the system; not having the right engineering specifications meant compromising building stability. But the Chinese supplier reduced the specifications anyway, until more than 10% of the aluminum weight was missing. There is little that foreign companies can do even if they catch the supplier. If they recall a product, it is done in their country—after having paid full prices before the product left China. Since supplier relationships are so difficult to find and foster in the first place, it is difficult to switch, and other suppliers may do the same. Supplier information is often kept as trade secrets, so it’s also hard to know by reputation. And when quality goes bad, it can tarnish not only your local but international reputation, as happened with McDonald’s and Yum Brand’s KFC, after a TV program revealed their Chinese supplier, Shanghai Husi Food Co., mixing in expired meat.

Key Takeaways

Even quality changes that may not seem like a big deal can accumulate over time. When quality issues are at hand, the hidden manufacturers don’t bear the burdens. The negative repercussions fall on the company who trusted those suppliers, so do your QC constantly and vigilantly.

HAAGEN DAZS SPECIALTY
DISTRIBUTION

And even if your manufacturers are good, consider your distributors carefully, especially if you have a specialty product. Haagen-Dasz bought specialty freezers and delivery trucks, but it’s not necessary to do everything yourself; we can help find you the best distributors for your particular needs.

The LITAO Approach to Quality Control
THE LITAO APPROACH
TO QUALITY CONTROL

An individual businessman sought to launch a private-label insulated coffee thermos to be sold on Amazon, with all production taking place in China. He had the brand, but needed quality manufacturing at European standards, not something China is known for.

The LITAO Approach:

Rigorous and systematic quality control was the biggest focus. We provided three best options of known direct suppliers; working with the manufacturers themselves—not resellers—allowed us to control production outcomes. In Europe, manufacturers are expected to comply with 100% of company requests, but in China, standards are often relaxed and products turn out in slightly different colors or even sizes; factories sometimes even start using different materials without informing the client. This might be okay for Chinese wholesalers, but not for us. Even after ordering samples and choosing a manufacturer, we hired a QC supervisor from Shanghai to go personally to the factories each week. Check-in points were implemented in all stages of production: samples during manufacturing, full QC pre-shipment, and supervision while loading—all while keeping to production schedule.

The Outcomes

For this product, we filtered up to 2% of goods produced with quality issues—including a batch with a misprinted logo—and pressured the factory into producing proper ones in only a few hours to make a shipment dead-line. Only careful vetting would ensure such fast and accurate turnaround. The client continues to order new batches, with sales profitable from the 3rd quarter and increasing. His line of production now includes not just a coffee thermos in various colors but also a glass bottle and a flask, all thoroughly QC-ed; other products are in development.

GroupOn : The Dangers of Not Localizing
GROUP ON: THE DANGERS
OF NOT LOCALIZING

GroupOn dominates much of the international market, but not China, despite entering with millions in investments and prominent Chinese partners. This was mostly a failure in long-term strategic thinking from the beginning: a strategy that sought to fit China to GroupOn’s international model instead of the other way around. Instead of using local expertise, GroupOn hired almost all-foreigner business school grads as top management (only two people on its senior management team were Chinese, and one was from Hong Kong). The company expected it could use the same strategy it did in Germany to poach salespeople from competitor companies via high salaries, but Chinese group-buying sites made an alliance such that if their employee left to work for GroupOn, they would no longer be able to work in the future in the Chinese alliance. It had looked rosy at the beginning: GroupOn was in league with two of China’s biggest tech companies, especially a 40% stake from Tencent (parent of the now-ubiquitous WeChat) and a smaller stake including Alibaba. But a non-compete clause was not included in their agreement, and both tech companies had investments in other Chinese group buying companies that ended up doing much better. GroupOn needed Tencent to operate in China much more than Tencent needed GroupOn for a group buying web-site, of which many already existed in China. GroupOn was even surprised when Lashou, China’s largest group-buying site, refused its huge offer to buy them. Simply having cash and latching on to large Chinese companies with conflicting interests is not a good localization strategy. This led to $46 million in net losses with only $2.1 million revenues within its first three quarters, and it has never recovered.

Nestle: A Good Will Strategy
NESTLE: A GOOD
WILL STRATEGY

After a massive earthquake hit western China in 2008, Nestlé donated 10 million yuan—no strings—to disaster relief. MOVING MONEY

With push from the Chinese government, the RMB is becoming increasingly internationalized. But it will still take a number of years before it shares equal status with the dollar, the euro, the pound, and the yen. Financial Fraud
FINANCIAL
FRAUD

Another thing to watch out for is financial fraud, something even the Chinese government has trouble controlling. A typical scam involves a regular Chinese supplier who asks you to make payments to a new bank account. Yet after you pay, your supplier says you still owe it the full amount, often with late fees, because it never received your payment. This scam can happen to the most experienced of companies, even to toy manufacturer Mattel, whose executives wired over USD 3 million to China’s Bank of Wenzhou after a fake e-mail which claimed to hold orders from the company president. Mattel acted quickly, alerting the FBI and Chinese authorities, and eventually got their money back, but most companies have no such luck. Sometimes they might convince the Chinese supplier to cover some of the costs—as it was their computer system that was compromised—but it depends on your supplier relationship. We recommend double-checking everything and putting in safety precautions like confirming payments on land line phone numbers which are much harder to hack, getting the supplier’s bank account information in advance, and having an internal corporate check system before large payments. Your Chinese suppliers will also understand, and perhaps even appreciate, the efforts you make for safety as they too lose out on scams.

Key Takeaways

Even huge experienced companies can fall to the simplest of financial schemes, so don’t think you’re exempt. Double-check and have a company policy to double-check each other and with whoever you’re paying, because organizational mistakes always happen on the individual level.
