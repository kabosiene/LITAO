TROUBLE WITH CUSTOMS COMPLIANCE

A US company’s product was denied entry by China’s inspection and quarantine bureau (AQSIQ) for six years straight. The bureau, which operates independently from the well-known customs administration (GACC), continually said the product was above its allowed limits for nitrate and nitrite—even though the importer and exporter tests both showed they were within limits. They finally had to hire a law firm to help them coordinate with a local inspection bureau so they could do identical product tests on both sides and compare results. However, the Chinese bureau’s standards allowed for only three testing methods, two of which weren’t even used in the US. They both agreed to do the tests using the remaining third method and identical materials, yielding results that finally allowed the company to ship to China. Without the law firm’s friendly relationship with the local inspection bureau, their products would still be languishing across the sea.

Key Takeaways
Even if you think you’re doing everything right, you may still be barred from entering China for reasons you can’t possibly know by yourself. Ask a local expert with connections. Inspection standards can vary depending on which testing methods are used.

A LONG PROCESS TO TRADE APPROVAL

Getting a product approved for trade can be a laborious process, even when your country’s highest leaders approve and get involved .

After Russian sanctions against EU products in 2014, Poland’s apple trade decreased almost 20% in a single year from 1.1 to 0.88 million tons. Russia had been 1/3 of its apple exports. To boost its apple trade, Poland applied for a trade agreement to import apples to China in August 2014.

Fresh vegetables and fruits are especially difficult to be approved for import to China; even though they usually have fewer risks of contamination, the Chinese government treated with politically weighty fruit import with the procedural caution usually reserved for animal products. All grower hygiene methods and qualities had to be adjusted and inspected, and each apple had to be traced back to its origins.

The trade agreement was finalized almost two years later in June 2016—this after a series of inspections, meetings between the two country’s heads of states, and after the Polish prime minister personally requested that the agreement be expedited. According to the Polish embassy in Beijing though, this was actually the FASTEST negotiation of this kind from EU states to the China market.

Key Takeaways
Regardless of the product, be prepared to wait at least a year for your product to be approved. If you’re not prepared for this, it could cause immense delays in your entry strategy. And remember: just meeting the regulations isn’t enough in China; it can also change them anytime. In this case, even though fruits and vegetables have less stringent quality inspection standards, the Chinese government treated them with the same standards as animal products. Regulations now are much stricter than a few decades ago, when China was just opening up and wanting to attract foreign companies. Now foreign companies are clamoring to enter, and China can pick and choose.
